## The mechanism

In several European public broadcast models, a familiar pattern appears: the **on-air personality** is also the **economic beneficiary** of the program's production entity. The network buys a show from a company the host controls or co-owns. Margins can reach **50–70%** on production fees — a structure that would be flagged in procurement law if it involved any other category of public spender.

## Structural conflict

| Role | Incentive |
|------|-----------|
| Host | Maximize audience conflict + personal brand |
| Producer (same person) | Maximize production invoice to the broadcaster |
| Public broadcaster | Fill schedule with proven ratings drivers |
| Taxpayer | Funds the loop |

## What the numbers suggest

Two private production societies have, at various points, accounted for a very large share of debate programming on major public channels. That is not proof of illegality — but it **is** proof of market concentration inside a public mandate.

## Editorial consequence

When economic interest and editorial authority collapse into the same individual, "debate" becomes a **revenue product**. The format selects guests, tempo, and conflict level that protect the franchise — not the public's need for falsifiable argument.

## Open questions for regulators

- Should host-producer dual roles trigger automatic competitive tender?
- Should public networks publish production margin bands by supplier?
- Should repeat suppliers face rotation mandates after N prime-time seasons?
