## Executive summary

France spends roughly **€4 billion per year** on public broadcasting. The official narrative presents this as a neutral public service. The operational reality — visible in procurement patterns, editorial calendars, and executive testimony — is a system optimized for narrative containment rather than adversarial scrutiny.

## What we investigated

- Whether editorial independence clauses in charter documents match scheduling and commissioning data
- How subcontracting chains obscure accountability between production companies and broadcast executives
- Whether "balance" metrics in annual reports correlate with guest diversity across political spectrum

## Key findings

1. **Neutrality is treated as branding, not governance.** Internal framing documents describe impartiality as an "aspirational editorial posture," not an enforceable standard.
2. **Commissioning concentration.** A small set of recurring production structures supplies a disproportionate share of prime-time debate formats.
3. **Subsidy without counterweight.** Taxpayer funding scales; mechanisms for independent audit of editorial impact do not.

## Why this matters

Democratic debate requires institutions that can fail loudly when power goes unchallenged. When public media functions as a stabilizing layer for incumbent narratives, the cost is not only financial — it is epistemic.
