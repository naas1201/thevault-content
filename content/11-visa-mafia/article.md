## Trigger event

In **Abuja, Nigeria**, inspectors raided a TLScontact office — one of the private contractors that process Schengen visa applications on behalf of European consulates. The episode connects that event to a broader pattern: **outsourced sovereignty**, where access to European territory is gated by profit-making intermediaries with weak transparency.

## The business model

1. Applicant pays service fee to contractor (often mandatory "premium" tiers)
2. Contractor controls appointment inventory
3. Passport often held during processing — **leverage point**
4. Complaints routed through contractor helpdesk, not consulate

## Documented pain points (cross-jurisdiction)

- Appointment scarcity engineered into upsell pathways
- Opaque refund rules on "value-added" services
- Data processing agreements that bundle unrelated marketing consent
- Asymmetric power: applicant cannot opt out of intermediary while using consular route

## Why "mafia" is not hyperbole

The term in the episode is rhetorical but structurally pointed: a closed system, territorial control of a required chokepoint, and financial extraction from people with no alternative provider.

## Policy angle

If visa processing is an extension of border policy, outsourcing it without **hard service-level enforcement** exports corruption risk into the Schengen brand itself.
